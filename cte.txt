Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE) is a progressive, degenerative brain disease caused by repeated head impacts, commonly in contact sports or military service. Key effects include severe cognitive decline (memory loss, dementia), mood disorders (depression, apathy), behavioral changes (aggression, impulsivity), and motor dysfunction. Symptoms often appear years or decades after injuries.

Key Effects of CTE
Cognitive Decline: Progressive loss of memory, difficulty with planning and organization, confusion, and impaired judgment.
Behavioral/Mood Changes: Increased impulsivity, aggression, irritability, depression, anxiety, and suicidal thoughts.
Physical/Motor Symptoms: Parkinsonism-like symptoms such as tremors, slow movement, and difficulties with balance and gait.
Neuropathological Changes: The disease causes brain tissue degeneration, specifically the accumulation of abnormal tau protein, leading to brain atrophy (shrinkage) in the frontal and temporal lobes.
Late-Stage Impact: As the disease advances, individuals may develop severe dementia.

Onset and Diagnosis
Symptoms often emerge 10 years or more after the initial trauma. There is currently no method to diagnose CTE in a living person; it can only be confirmed post-mortem by analyzing brain tissue.

Risk Factors
CTE is primarily associated with long-term, repeated head trauma, including sub-concussive hits, commonly experienced in sports such as football, boxing, and rugby, as well as in military personnel.
